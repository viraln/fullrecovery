### Reaction-Diffusion Patterns: The Turing Mechanism

One of the most influential theories explaining pattern formation is the Turing mechanism, proposed by Alan Turing in 1952. This mechanism describes how "reaction-diffusion patterns" can arise from the interaction of two or more chemical substances that react with each other and diffuse at different rates. One substance acts as an activator, promoting its own production and that of an inhibitor. The inhibitor, in turn, suppresses the activator. Because of the difference in diffusion rates, the activator tends to form local clusters, while the inhibitor spreads out more evenly, preventing runaway activation and leading to stable, spatially varying patterns. These "Turing patterns" are fundamental to understanding morphogenesis, the biological process that governs the development of an organism's shape.
