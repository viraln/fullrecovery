## How Does Fusion Power Work?

Unlike fission, which splits atoms, fusion combines them.  Specifically, fusion power utilizes the process of fusing light atomic nuclei, most commonly isotopes of hydrogen (deuterium and tritium), to create helium. This reaction releases an immense amount of energy â€“ far greater than fission â€“ due to the conversion of a small amount of mass into energy, as described by Einstein's famous equation, E=mcÂ².  ![Diagram illustrating the fusion process of deuterium and tritium](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1466629437334-b4f6603563c5?q=85&w=1200&fit=max&fm=webp&auto=compress)

The process requires incredibly high temperatures and pressures to overcome the electrostatic repulsion between the positively charged nuclei. This is achieved within a fusion reactor, a complex device designed to confine and heat the plasma (a superheated state of matter) to the necessary conditions for fusion to occur.  Several approaches exist, including magnetic confinement (like in tokamaks) and inertial confinement (using lasers).

## Types of Fusion Reactors and Current Developments

Several designs compete for dominance in the fusion reactor landscape.  Tokamaks, donut-shaped devices using powerful magnetic fields to contain plasma, are currently the most advanced.  ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor), a massive international collaboration in France, is a prime example, aiming to demonstrate the scientific and technological feasibility of fusion energy.  ![Image of the ITER tokamak under construction](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1515191107209-c28698631303?q=85&w=1200&fit=max&fm=webp&auto=compress)

Other designs include stellarators (with more complex magnetic field configurations for improved plasma confinement), and inertial confinement fusion (ICF), where powerful lasers implode tiny fuel pellets to initiate fusion.
