## What is Immune System Computing?

Immune system computing (ISC), also known as artificial immune systems (AIS), draws inspiration from the biological immune system's mechanisms to develop robust and adaptive computational solutions. Unlike traditional computing approaches, ISC emphasizes distributed, self-organizing systems capable of learning and adapting to dynamic environments.  It mimics the key aspects of the immune system, including:

* 🔑 **Antigen recognition:** Identifying and classifying threats (data anomalies, malicious code, etc.).
* ⚡ **Antibody generation:** Creating solutions or responses to identified threats.
* 🔄 **Clonal selection:**  Favorably selecting and refining effective solutions.
* ♻️ **Memory:** Storing past experiences to improve future responses.
* 🔄 **Self/non-self discrimination:** Distinguishing between benign and harmful data.

## Core AIS Algorithms and Techniques

Several algorithms form the foundation of AIS.  These algorithms leverage the principles outlined above to solve various computational problems. Some prominent examples include:

* **Negative Selection Algorithm (NSA):** This algorithm focuses on generating detectors (analogous to antibodies) that react to "non-self" patterns, effectively identifying anomalies or intrusions.
* **Clonal Selection Algorithm (CSA):** This algorithm mimics the process of clonal expansion and affinity maturation, refining solutions through iterative improvements.
* **Immune Network Algorithm (INA):** This algorithm models the interactions between different immune cells, creating a complex network for problem-solving.
* **Artificial Immune Recognition System (AIRS):** This approach combines various techniques, often incorporating machine learning elements for enhanced performance.

Choosing the right algorithm depends heavily on the specific problem being addressed.  Understanding the strengths and weaknesses of each is crucial for successful implementation.
