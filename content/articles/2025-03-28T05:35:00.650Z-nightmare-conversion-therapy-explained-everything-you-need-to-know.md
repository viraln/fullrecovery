## Understanding the Core: What is Conversion Therapy?

Conversion therapy, also known as reparative therapy, is a range of dangerous and discredited practices falsely claiming to change a person’s sexual orientation or gender identity. These practices are based on the misguided belief that being lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, or queer (LGBTQ+) is a mental illness that can be "cured."

These practices can include:

*   Counseling aimed at changing sexual orientation or gender identity
*   Aversive techniques, such as electric shock or nausea-inducing drugs, paired with same-sex attractions
*   Efforts to suppress gender expression
*   Spiritual interventions, such as exorcism
*   Talk therapy focused on altering thoughts, feelings, and behaviors

> **EXPERT TIP:** It's crucial to remember that conversion therapy has been debunked by every major medical and mental health organization. There is no scientific evidence to support its effectiveness, and it is widely recognized as harmful.

The core problem is that conversion therapy operates on the flawed premise that LGBTQ+ identities are inherently wrong or undesirable. This belief system, often rooted in religious or cultural biases, fuels the drive to "correct" individuals, leading to immense psychological damage.

## The Devastating Reality: Conversion Therapy Trauma

The immediate and long-term effects of conversion therapy can be devastating. Survivors often experience severe psychological distress, including:

*   **Increased risk of suicide:** Studies show a significantly higher rate of suicide attempts among those who have undergone conversion therapy.
*   **Depression and anxiety:** The shame, guilt, and self-loathing instilled by these practices can lead to chronic mental health issues.
*   **Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD):** The traumatic nature of conversion therapy can trigger flashbacks, nightmares, and hypervigilance.
*   **Self-harm:** Feelings of worthlessness and hopelessness can lead to self-injurious behaviors.
*   **Substance abuse:** As a coping mechanism for the emotional pain, some survivors turn to drugs and alcohol.
*   **Difficulty with intimacy and relationships:** The trauma can make it difficult to form healthy attachments and trust others.

Conversion therapy survivors often grapple with a profound sense of betrayal, especially if the therapy was imposed by family members or religious leaders. The damage to their self-esteem and sense of identity can be long-lasting and require extensive therapy to heal.
