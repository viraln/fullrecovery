## What is Quadratic Voting?

Quadratic voting (QV) is a novel voting mechanism designed to amplify the voices of individuals with strong opinions while mitigating the impact of strategic voting and manipulation. Unlike traditional voting where each person gets one vote, QV allows individuals to allocate a fixed budget of "voting points" across multiple options.  The crucial difference lies in the *cost* of expressing preferences: the more points allocated to a single option, the higher the cost per point. This quadratic scaling means that casting many votes for a single item becomes increasingly expensive, preventing individuals from dominating the outcome.

> **Did you know?**  The concept of quadratic voting was initially proposed by economist Glen Weyl and mathematician Vitalik Buterin (co-founder of Ethereum) in 2016, and has since gained significant traction in various fields.

## How Does Quadratic Voting Work?

Let's break down the process with a practical example:

Imagine a community deciding on three projects: a new park, a community center, and improved roads.  Each member receives, say, 10 voting points.

1. **Allocation:** Each member distributes their 10 points across the three projects. They might allocate 5 points to the park, 3 to the community center, and 2 to roads.
2. **Square Root Calculation:**  For each project, the system calculates the square root of the total points allocated to it. This is where the "quadratic" aspect comes in.
3. **Weighted Vote:**  The square root value represents the project's weighted vote. The project with the highest weighted vote wins.

This process ensures that individuals with strong preferences (allocating many points to one project) have a proportionally greater influence than those with weak preferences (spreading points thinly).  However, it prevents individuals from completely dominating the outcome by making it exponentially more expensive to cast many votes for a single option.
