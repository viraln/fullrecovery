## Understanding the Fundamentals of Light Field Technology

Light field technology, at its core, is about capturing more information about light than traditional cameras. A conventional camera records only the intensity of light hitting each pixel on its sensor. A light field camera, however, captures both the intensity *and* the direction of light rays. This additional directional information, also known as angular information, is what unlocks the incredible capabilities of light field computing. This is often achieved through plenoptic imaging.

![Illustration comparing how a traditional camera captures light versus a light field camera capturing both intensity and direction.](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1529310399831-ed472b81d589?q=85&w=1200&fit=max&fm=webp&auto=compress)

Plenoptic imaging is the process of capturing the entire light field. Think of it as recording all the light rays emanating from a scene. This data-rich representation of light allows for a multitude of post-capture manipulations, including refocusing, depth estimation, and viewpoint shifting, all computationally.

Light field cameras achieve this feat through various methods, most commonly by incorporating an array of microlenses placed in front of the main image sensor. These microlenses effectively create a multitude of tiny lenses, each capturing a slightly different perspective of the scene. The data from these micro-lenses is then processed using sophisticated light field image processing algorithms.

## Light Field Image Processing: The Computational Backbone
